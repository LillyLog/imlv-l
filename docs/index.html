1 Introduction

1.1 Project Overview

This book presents a comprehensive analysis of traffic congestion patterns in New York City using machine learning interpretability methods. Traffic congestion is a significant challenge in urban areas like NYC, affecting daily commutes, emergency response times, economic activity, and environmental quality. By applying advanced interpretability techniques such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), and feature importance metrics, we aim to gain insights into the factors that influence traffic patterns.

1.2 Research Questions

The primary questions this research seeks to answer include:

  1. What are the key factors that affect traffic congestion in NYC?
  2. Do different interpretability methods (SHAP, LIME, feature importance) identify consistent factors?
  3. How stable are these interpretations across different model types and data splits?
  4. Can these insights inform policy decisions related to traffic management?

1.3 Significance

Understanding the drivers of urban traffic congestion has several important applications:

  • Traffic Management: Insights can help optimize traffic signal timing, lane configurations, and other operational aspects.
  • Urban Planning: Identifying key congestion factors can inform future infrastructure development.
  • Policy Evaluation: Data-driven assessment of congestion-reducing policies.
  • Emergency Services: Improved prediction of traffic conditions for emergency response planning.

1.4 Data Sources

This analysis integrates multiple data sources:

  • Traffic Volume Data: High-resolution traffic count data from NYC Department of Transportation
  • Weather Data: Historical temperature and precipitation information
  • Emergency Response Data: Response time metrics as indicators of road network performance

1.5 Methodology

Our analytical approach follows these steps:

  1. Data Preprocessing: Cleaning, integration, and feature engineering of diverse datasets
  2. Exploratory Analysis: Visualization and statistical analysis of traffic patterns
  3. Model Development: Training multiple machine learning models to predict traffic volumes
  4. Interpretability Analysis: Applying and comparing SHAP, LIME, and feature importance techniques
  5. Stability Analysis: Assessing the robustness of interpretations across different conditions

1.6 Book Structure

The remainder of this book is organized as follows:

  • Chapter 2: Data - Description of data sources, cleaning procedures, and feature engineering
  • Chapter 3: Analysis - Methodological approach, model development, and interpretability techniques
  • Chapter 4: Results - Findings from the interpretability analysis and comparison of methods
  • Chapter 5: Conclusion - Summary of insights and recommendations for future research
